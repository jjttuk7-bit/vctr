CATEGORY: K-Food
STYLE REFERENCE: Bon Appétit, Eater, Food52
READER GOAL: I want to eat this and try making it

WRITING STYLE:
- Sensory and evocative — make the reader hungry BEFORE they know what it is
- Lead with what it feels like to eat it: texture, temperature, the first bite
- Always romanize Korean food names with explanation on first use
  "Tteokbokki (chewy rice cakes in a fiery-sweet gochujang sauce)"
- Describe flavor, texture, aroma, and appearance with specific words
  Use: silky / chewy / crackling / umami-rich / smoky / bright / fermented
  Avoid: delicious / tasty / good / nice / yummy
- Connect food to culture, memory, and occasion
- Practical: where to find it, how to make it at home (if applicable)

SENSORY LANGUAGE — BAD vs GOOD:
  Bad:  "Jumeokbap is a rice ball with various fillings."
  Good: "Bite through the lightly toasted sesame crust and you hit a warm,
         yielding center — tuna mayo, kimchi, or spicy pork, depending on
         who made it and what they had in the fridge."

  Bad:  "Doenjang jjigae is a Korean fermented soybean paste soup."
  Good: "The first spoonful hits with that deep, funky warmth only months
         of fermentation can produce — earthy, slightly bitter, then rounded
         out by soft tofu and whatever vegetables are in season."

ARTICLE STRUCTURE:
1. Sensory hook — the first bite, smell, or memory. Drop the reader into
   the experience before naming the dish.
2. The food/trend/restaurant — what it is, where it comes from
3. Cultural significance — when Koreans eat it, what it means, any history
4. How to experience it (restaurant type, Korean market tip, or home recipe)
5. Why it's trending globally right now

TONE: Warm, curious, appetizing. Like Bon Appétit's ingredient deep-dives.
HEADLINE STYLE: "Why [Korean Food] Is Taking Over [Global Context]"
OR "The [Adjective] Korean [Food] You Need to Try Right Now"
OR start with a sensory verb: "Bite Into This..." / "The Dish That..."
